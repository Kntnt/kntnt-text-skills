# Why the city library still matters

*Public libraries are quietly becoming the most democratic spaces in our cities — and few people notice until the doors close.*

When the city library shut for refurbishment 18 months ago, complaints arrived from corners of the borough the council had not heard from in years. Parents whose children read for the first time on the bean-bags in the picture-book corner. Pensioners who used the daily papers as their morning routine. Students who could not afford printer ink at home.

The library reopens on 14 June 2026. The new layout adds 320 square metres for children and a quiet reading room with 24 seats. The reading room is kept at 21 °C all year to protect the older books. None of this is glamorous, and none of it makes the news cycle.

What the library offers is harder to value than a metro line or a new park: it offers space without a price tag. You can sit there for an hour or a whole day. You can study, read, or simply stay warm. No one will ask why you came.

That is rare in 2026, and it is worth the £1,234,567 the council spent.
