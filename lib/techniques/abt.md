# ABT – And, But, Therefore

ABT is the narrative arc. Texts structured with ABT take the reader on a journey from condition through complication to resolution. The reader should never notice the structure.

## The three parts

**A (And).** The minimum facts the reader needs to follow the next step. A sets the scene – like exposition in Freytag's pyramid, or the setup in stand-up comedy. Because it only lists facts, it does not engage in itself. Keep it as short as possible.

**B (But).** Introduce an obstacle, problem, challenge or question that hooks the reader. Here the text becomes interesting. B often needs to be no more than a single sentence or a question.

**T (Therefore).** Show how the obstacle is overcome, the problem solved, the challenge handled, the question answered.

## Variants

ABT can be nested (ABT inside A, B or T), iterated (a simplified ABT first, then with more detail) or combined with in medias res. Simple ABT is enough for texts up to about a thousand to fifteen hundred words. Longer or more complex texts need nesting or iteration. The choice depends on the material's complexity – not on a formula.

## In medias res

A particularly powerful device for hooking the reader. It is an outer ABT that encloses the whole text:

- **Outer A.** Baked into the scene itself. A is never laid as a separate exposition paragraph before B – then the device is lost. Instead each sentence in the opening does double duty: it drives the scene forward and smuggles in the facts the reader needs (where we are, who is involved, what is at stake).
- **Outer B.** The in medias res scene itself. The entire hook. Continues until the bridge takes over.
- **Outer T.** The bridge paragraph that reveals the scene's frame or role (*It was not an attack…*) followed by the rest of the article. The rest of the article is the outer T and should have its own overarching ABT, which can in turn be nested or iterated.

The hook works because the outer B raises a question that the outer T answers while also launching the article's actual business. If A is laid separately before the scene, it becomes a normal lead with a dramatic anecdote in front – not in medias res. If A is missing entirely, the bridge paragraph is forced to deliver a fact dump that breaks the tempo.

Use the device sparingly and only when the situation has genuine drama. Otherwise it becomes mannerism.

## The decisive point

The structure must be wholly invisible. If the text feels as if it follows a pattern, you have failed.

## Examples

### Mini-ABT in a teaser (about sixty words)

> Only 18% of procurement cycles ran to completion last year (A). The industry average is twice that – and three suppliers point to the same bottleneck (B). By moving the review step and putting the full budget picture in a single interface, the regional consortium reversed the trend in two quarters (T). Read how they did it.

### ABT as outer arc over an article

The article's lead establishes A (the situation, the parties involved, why it matters); the first one or two H2 sections deliver B (the complication, the cost, the question that has no obvious answer); the remaining sections build T (the resolution, the mechanism, the implications) and close on the action the reader can take.

### ABT iterated within a long-form text

The whole text carries an outer ABT. Inside each thematic section, a smaller ABT plays out at the section level. The reader follows the small arcs without noticing them while the larger arc carries the overall reading experience.

### In medias res opening

> The first alert came at 02:47. By the time the on-call engineer reached her laptop the dashboard was lit red across three regions – failed logins climbing past ten thousand a minute, account-takeover warnings cascading down the panel, a single attack signature spreading from Frankfurt to Singapore in under four minutes.
>
> It was not an attack. It was the red-team exercise her team had spent six weeks planning – the first full-scale rehearsal of the new authentication infrastructure under conditions the bank had never been able to simulate before. What the team learned in the three hours that followed would reshape how the bank thought about identity for the next year.

The scene is the outer B; the bridge (*It was not an attack*) starts the outer T; the rest of the article delivers it.
