# PAC — Premise, Analysis, Conclusion

PAC is the analytical arc. Texts structured with PAC walk the reader through evidence and let her reach the conclusion at the end of the path. The structure is permitted — even welcomed — to be visible at the section level, because the genres that use PAC (chiefly report) reward a reader who can follow the reasoning step by step.

## The three parts

**P (Premise).** The data, historical context, or starting point the analysis builds on. The premise establishes what we have in front of us — the figures, the source material, the situation as it presents itself. The premise is descriptive: this is what is the case.

**A (Analysis).** The mechanism, the finding, what the premise actually means. The analysis explains the why — why the numbers look as they do, what pattern they fit, what hypothesis they support or rule out. The analysis is interpretive: this is what the premise tells us when we look closely.

**C (Conclusion).** The recommendation, the implication, or the answer to the question the section was investigating. The conclusion is positional: given this premise and this analysis, this is what follows. In a report, the conclusion may be a recommendation, an implication for strategy, or a marker that further investigation is needed.

## Where PAC applies

PAC is the default technique for report (see `lib/genres/report.md`). It typically structures each large thematic section within a report rather than the report as a whole — the report's overall shape is given by the genre (introduction, body sections, optional executive summary), and PAC sits inside those sections.

PAC can also be used inside an article or web-copy section when the material is genuinely analytical and the reader benefits from following the reasoning step by step. It is rarely the right choice for column or opinion, where ABT serves the genre better.

## Visibility

Unlike ABT, PAC tolerates being visible. A reader who has come to a report or analytical section expects to see the path from data to conclusion — that visibility is part of the report's credibility. The danger is not visibility but mechanicalness. Section headings should not be labelled *P:*, *A:*, *C:*. The premise is named for what it contains (*Traffic fell during Q1*), the analysis for what it found (*The loss comes from thirty search terms*), the conclusion for what it claims (*Content production needs to refocus*).

## Variants

PAC can be nested — a section's overall PAC can contain sub-sections that each carry their own PAC at a finer granularity. PAC can also be partial: a section may carry only premise and analysis when the conclusion follows later in the document, or only analysis and conclusion when the premise is established elsewhere and referenced.

## The decisive point

The order matters. Evidence before conclusion (see `rules/style.md`). A section that delivers the conclusion before the premise has skipped the analytical work — the reader is asked to trust rather than to follow. In a report, that breaks the credibility pact.

## Examples

### PAC in a quarterly SEO report section

> ### Organic traffic fell by 12 per cent during Q1
>
> Organic traffic to the website reached 184,000 sessions during the first quarter of 2026 (P). That is a 12 per cent decrease compared with Q1 2025 and deviates from the trend across the four preceding quarters, when volume grew by 4 to 7 per cent quarter on quarter.
>
> The loss is concentrated in around thirty search terms close to purchase intent. Search ranking for those terms has fallen by an average of 4.2 positions since December (A). The pattern matches the one described in Google's March 2026 update: sites with thin product information lose visibility on purchase-oriented queries.
>
> Content production for the second quarter should prioritise deepening the thirty pages most affected (C). The volume loss can be recovered within two quarters if the content meets Google's product-information guidelines.

### PAC nested inside a larger section

The section's outer PAC presents an overall premise (the data picture for the quarter), an overall analysis (the dominant pattern), and an overall conclusion (the strategic recommendation). Inside, individual subsections handle smaller PACs — one per data slice — that together build the analysis the outer section concludes from.

### Partial PAC: Premise and Analysis only

When the conclusion belongs in a later, summative section, an earlier section may carry only P and A. The reader knows from the report's structure that conclusions are pulled together at the end; the section's job is to lay the groundwork.
